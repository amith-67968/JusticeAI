RENT CONTROL AND TENANCY LAWS IN INDIA
========================================

Tenancy law in India is governed by a combination of central legislation and state-specific rent control acts. The key central law is the Transfer of Property Act, 1882.

TRANSFER OF PROPERTY ACT, 1882 — KEY SECTIONS
----------------------------------------------

Section 105 — Lease Defined:
A lease of immoveable property is a transfer of a right to enjoy such property made for a certain time, express or implied, or in perpetuity, in consideration of a price paid or promised, or of money, a share of crops, service or any other thing of value to be rendered periodically or on specified occasions to the transferor (lessor/landlord) by the transferee (lessee/tenant).

Section 106 — Duration and Termination of Lease:
- Agricultural or manufacturing lease: year-to-year, terminable by 6 months' notice
- Other purposes: month-to-month, terminable by 15 days' notice
- Notice must expire with the end of a month of the tenancy

Section 107 — Lease How Made:
A lease of immoveable property from year to year, or for any term exceeding one year, can only be made by a registered instrument. All other leases may be made either by registered instrument or by oral agreement accompanied by delivery of possession.

Section 108 — Rights and Liabilities of Lessor and Lessee:
- Lessor must disclose material defects
- Lessor is bound to put the lessee in possession
- Lessee must pay rent and keep the property in good condition
- Lessee must not use the property for purposes other than those for which it was leased

Section 111 — Determination of Lease:
A lease may be determined by:
(a) Efflux of time
(b) Happening of a specified event
(c) Mutual agreement
(d) Notice to quit
(e) Forfeiture (breach of condition)
(f) Merger

TENANT RIGHTS
-------------

1. Right to Due Process: A tenant cannot be evicted without following the due process of law (court order). Self-help eviction (locking out, cutting water/electricity) by the landlord is illegal.

2. Right to Essential Services: Landlord cannot disconnect water, electricity, or other essential services to force eviction. This may attract criminal penalties under state rent control acts.

3. Right to Fair Rent: Under most state rent control laws, rent increasescannot be arbitrary. The Rent Controller can fix "standard rent" or "fair rent."

4. Right to Receipt: Tenant has the right to receive a rent receipt from the landlord for every payment made.

5. Right to Privacy: Landlord must give reasonable notice before entering the rented premises, except in emergencies.

LANDLORD RIGHTS
---------------

1. Right to Eviction for Non-Payment: If the tenant defaults on rent for a specified period (usually 3 months), the landlord can file for eviction.

2. Right to Eviction for Personal Need: Landlord can seek eviction if they genuinely need the premises for personal use or for the use of family members.

3. Right to Eviction for Misuse: If the tenant uses the premises for a purpose other than what was agreed upon, the landlord can seek eviction.

4. Right to Fair Rent: Landlord can apply for revision of rent if the current rent is below the fair market rate (under state rent control acts).

5. Right to Property Return: Upon termination of the lease, the tenant must return the property in the same condition (subject to normal wear and tear).

EVICTION PROCESS
----------------

Step 1: Send a legal notice to the tenant specifying the ground for eviction and giving them reasonable time to comply (usually 15 days to 1 month).

Step 2: If the tenant does not vacate, file an eviction petition before the Rent Controller or competent civil court.

Step 3: The court will issue notice to the tenant and hear both parties. The tenant can contest the eviction.

Step 4: If the court is satisfied that grounds for eviction exist, it will pass an eviction order. The tenant is usually given 30-90 days to vacate.

MODEL TENANCY ACT, 2021
------------------------

The central government published the Model Tenancy Act (MTA) in 2021 for adoption by states. Key provisions:

1. Security Deposit Cap:
   - Residential: Maximum 2 months' rent
   - Commercial: Maximum 6 months' rent

2. Rent Revision: Not more than once during a tenancy agreement, with 3 months' prior notice.

3. Written Agreement: Every tenancy must have a written agreement, which must be submitted to the Rent Authority within 2 months.

4. Notice Period:
   - Tenant vacating: Must give notice equal to the rent payment period (e.g., 1 month if paying monthly)
   - Landlord: Must give notice as specified in the agreement

5. Rent Court & Tribunal: Establishes dedicated Rent Courts and Rent Tribunals for quick dispute resolution.

6. Anti-Subletting: Tenant cannot sublease or transfer the premises without written consent of the landlord.

7. Overstaying Penalty: If a tenant does not vacate after the tenancy period ends and notice has been given:
   - First 2 months: Double the monthly rent
   - After 2 months: 4 times the monthly rent
